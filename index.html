Covariance Computation & Analytical Interpretation

Bivariate sample covariances calculated across 1,000 corporate bond records using $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{n-1}$.

Covariance Matrix

Variable Pair Formula Notation Computed Value Direction
($X_1$, $X_2$) D/E vs Coverage $\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)$ -0.4893 Negative
($X_2$, $X_3$) Coverage vs Yield $\text{Cov}(X_2, X_3)$ -0.8545 Strong Negative

Interpretation of Cov(X1, X2) = -0.4893

The negative covariance confirms that financial leverage and debt-service buffer move in opposite directions. Firms taking on more leverage ($X_1$) experience lower safety margins in covering interest expenses ($X_2$).

Interpretation of Cov(X2, X3) = -0.8545

Strong negative covariance shows that high interest coverage ($X_2$) signifies strong credit health and low default probability, allowing the firm to issue bonds at significantly lower coupon yield rates ($X_3$).

Interpretation of Cov(X1, X3) = +0.3740

Positive covariance demonstrates risk-premium pricing. Bondholders demand higher yield compensation ($X_3$) when investing in highly leveraged corporate issuers ($X_1$).

Task 4(ii)

Chebyshev's Inequality Bounds & Variability Analysis

Chebyshev's inequality states that for any random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and finite standard deviation $\sigma$, and for any constant $k > 1$:

P(|X - μ| < kσ) ≥ 1 - 1/k²  |  P(μ - kσ ≤ X ≤ μ + kσ) ≥ 1 - 1/k²

1. Standard 2-Sigma Bound ($k = 2 \implies 1 - 1/2^2 = 75\%$ Minimum Probability)

Variable Mean ($\mu$) Std Dev ($\sigma$) Lower Bound ($\mu - 2\sigma$) Upper Bound ($\mu + 2\sigma$) Theoretical Prob Empirical Proportion
$X_1$ (Debt to Equity) 1.7913 0.4884 0.8144 2.7682 $\ge 75.00\%$ 95.50% (955/1000)
$X_2$ (Interest Coverage) 4.7275 1.1548 2.4178 7.0371 $\ge 75.00\%$ 95.50% (955/1000)
$X_3$ (Bond Yield %) 2.9182 0.8796 1.1590% 4.6774% $\ge 75.00\%$ 95.90% (959/1000)
Formulated Statement for X1 (Debt-to-Equity Ratio):

By applying Chebyshev's inequality with $k=2$, we can be at least 75% sure that a randomly selected corporate bond's Debt-to-Equity ratio ($X_1$) will lie between 0.8144 and 2.7682 (or $P(0.8144 \le X_1 \le 2.7682) \ge 0.75$). Consequently, the probability that $X_1$ is less than 0.8144 or greater than 2.7682 is at most 0.25 (25%).

Formulated Statement for X2 (Interest Coverage Ratio):

By applying Chebyshev's inequality with $k=2$, we can be at least 75% sure that a corporate bond's Interest Coverage ratio ($X_2$) will lie between 2.4178 and 7.0371 (or $P(2.4178 \le X_2 \le 7.0371) \ge 0.75$). The probability of observing an extreme ratio outside this interval is at most 0.25.

Formulated Statement for X3 (Bond Yield %):

By applying Chebyshev's inequality with $k=2$, we can be at least 75% sure that a bond's yield percentage ($X_3$) will lie between 1.1590% and 4.6774% (or $P(1.1590 \le X_3 \le 4.6774) \ge 0.75$). The probability that yield exceeds 4.6774% or falls below 1.1590% is at most 0.25.

2. 3-Sigma Bound ($k = 3 \implies 1 - 1/3^2 = 88.89\%$ Minimum Probability)

Variable Lower Bound ($\mu - 3\sigma$) Upper Bound ($\mu + 3\sigma$) Theoretical Guarantee Empirical Proportion
$X_1$ (Debt to Equity) 0.3260 3.2566 $\ge 88.89\%$ 99.60% (996/1000)
$X_2$ (Interest Coverage) 1.2630 8.1920 $\ge 88.89\%$ 99.60% (996/1000)
$X_3$ (Bond Yield %) 0.2794% 5.5570% $\ge 88.89\%$ 99.40% (994/1000)
Significance of Bounds & Distribution Behavior

Chebyshev's inequality provides a universal, distribution-free lower bound regardless of whether variables are normally distributed or skewed. Empirical observation on our 1,000-bond dataset reveals that ~95.5% of data points lie within $\pm 2\sigma$ and ~99.5% lie within $\pm 3\sigma$, comfortably exceeding Chebyshev's conservative theoretical guarantees of 75% and 88.89% respectively.
